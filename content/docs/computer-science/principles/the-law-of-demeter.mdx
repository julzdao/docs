## Core Principle

It recommends that objects should avoid accessing
the internal data and methods of other objects.

To put this more clearly, an object should only invoke methods on:

* Itself.
* Its direct dependencies (fields or instance variables).
* Objects passed in as arguments.
* Objects it creates.

<ObsidianCallout type="info">
  <ObsidianCalloutTitle>
    Explanation
  </ObsidianCalloutTitle>

  <ObsidianCalloutBody>
    An object should only concern itself with its own affairs or those that truly pertain to it. It should not know what is happening beyond its own life. This is a way for the object to be at peace and have as little information as possible about what is going on around it.
  </ObsidianCalloutBody>
</ObsidianCallout>

By following the Law of Demeter, you often naturally create classes with more focused responsibilities, thus supporting the [Single Responsibility Principle](./single-responsibility-principle.mdx). Conversely, when a class adheres to SRP, it's often easier to follow the Law of Demeter because it has a well-defined boundary and fewer reasons to reach out to distant objects.

## Benefits

1. **Loose Coupling**: Changing a part of the system will not break another part.
2. [Encapsulation](../java/encapsulation.mdx) : Each class is responsible for its own logic.
3. **Better Maintainability**: Less knowledge about other objects’ structures means fewer dependencies.

<ObsidianCallout type="info">
  <ObsidianCalloutTitle>
    History
  </ObsidianCalloutTitle>

  <ObsidianCalloutBody>
    The Law of Demeter was proposed by Ian Holland in 1987 at Northeastern University as part of the *Demeter Project*, an adaptive programming and aspect-oriented programming effort. The project was named after Demeter, the Greek goddess of agriculture, to signify a bottom-up philosophy of programming that is also embodied in the law itself.
  </ObsidianCalloutBody>
</ObsidianCallout>
